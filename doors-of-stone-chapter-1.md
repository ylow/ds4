# THE DOORS OF STONE

*Book Three of the Kingkiller Chronicles*

---

**PROLOGUE**

**A Silence of Three Shadows**

The third silence fell upon the Waystone Inn as the last ember of the second day's fire died among the ashes.

It was a silence of three shadows.

The first shadow was the shadow of the man behind the bar. He had been many things in his time — a storm, a flame, a broken blade — but now he was an innkeeper, and the silence that hung around him was the silence of a man who had stopped telling his own story because the ending was already written and he could not bear to speak it aloud.

The second shadow was the shadow of the young man who had sat listening. He had come seeking a story, and he had found one, and now the weight of it pressed against him like a stone. He did not move. He did not speak. He merely sat in the hollow of the silence and let it settle around him like dust.

The third shadow was the shadow of the moon. It had no face, no voice, no name that could be spoken aloud without consequence. It was older than the others, deeper, and it touched everything — the tarnished copper of the penny-coin, the cracked leather of the bar-stool, the pale skin of the innkeeper's hands. It was the silence of a thing that was broken and could not be fixed.

Outside, the wind moved through the empty streets of Newarre like a man searching for something he had lost. The stars were hard and bright above the rooftops, and the moon was a sliver of silver, a fingernail paring, a ghost of what it had once been.

There was no sound of laughter. No sound of song. The Waystone Inn was a hollow place, and the silence that lived there had three shadows, and they were as deep as the water at the bottom of the world.

**CHAPTER 1**

**The Weight of Stones**

The fire had burned down to ash and coal. I watched the last of the light die, and I did not move to feed it.

"It's late," said Bast, and I heard the concern in his voice buried beneath layers of careful calm. "Rese. We should stop for the night. Chronicler needs to rest. You need to rest."

I shook my head slowly, still staring at the gray ash. "No."

"Rese—"

"*No.*" I did not look at him. I could not. The story was a wound, and if I stopped speaking, I feared the bleeding would begin again.

Chronicler stirred in his chair, the scratch of his pen falling silent. He had been a patient man through all of this — through the long days and longer nights, through the wine and the whiskey, through the slow unspooling of a life I had tried to bury. But now I saw him lean forward, his eyes sharp with a hunger that had not dimmed through all the hours of telling.

"There is a door," I said, and the words hung in the air like smoke.

Bast made a sound low in his throat. "Rese, *please*—"

"There is a door in the world," I said, louder now, and I did not recognize my own voice. "And I have walked to it. I have stood before it. I have opened it."

The silence that followed was not comfortable. It was sharp, like the edge of a blade held against skin.

Bast stared at me, his expression unreadable. Chronicler's pen hovered above the page. The night pressed against the windows, dark and patient, waiting.

I looked at them — my student and my chronicler — and I felt the story rise in me like a tide that would not be held back.

"I have opened it," I said, and my voice was not the voice of Kote the innkeeper. It was the voice of a man who had once known the names of things. "And I will tell you what I found there."

Bast stood so fast he knocked his chair over. "No. *No.*" The word came out sharp and hard, and for a moment he was not a young man with soft hands and a pretty face. He was something older, something fierce, something that had lived too long and seen too much to be surprised — but was surprised anyway. "You cannot. You swore to me. You *swore*."

"Bast," I said, and my voice was tired, "there are things you do not know."

"I know enough." He was pacing now, the shadow of his true shape flickering at the edges of the lamplight. "I know what waits in that place. I know what you brought back. I know what it cost you. And I know what it will cost the world if you speak it again."

"Then you know more than I do," I said, and I felt the bitter truth of the words as I spoke them. "Because I have forgotten more than I remember, and the things I remember are not the things I wish to forget."

Chronicler cleared his throat, and the sound was small and human in the space between Bast's fury and my silence. "I have followed your story through two days and two books' worth of telling," he said. "I have heard about the Chandrian, and the Amyr, and the four-plate door. I have heard about the Lackless family, and the box, and the moon's name. I have heard about a woman you loved and could not save. But I have not heard the ending. And I cannot write an ending I do not know."

"Then you will write an ending," Bast said, rounding on him, "and you will write it here, in this place, with the shadows you have gathered and the names you have forgotten. You will write a pretty lie and you will call it a story and you will go home to your people across the water and you will leave us to our silence."

"I cannot write a lie," Chronicler said. "I am a scribe. I write what I hear, and if I do not hear it, I write nothing at all. That is the only truth I have ever given the world."

I watched them argue — the immortal and the historian, each fighting over a story that was already written in blood and bone. And I felt something I had not felt in a long time.

I felt the shape of a name forming in my mouth.

Not a calling name. Not a naming name. The name of a story, the way it begins and the way it ends. The name of a door I had walked through and a door I had closed behind me.

I stood.

They both fell silent.

"Tomorrow," I said. "At dawn. I will finish what I have begun. And when the sun sets tomorrow, you will know everything I have done and everything I have failed to do."

Bast opened his mouth, but I did not hear what he said, because I was already walking toward the door to the back rooms, and the silence of the Waystone Inn was no longer a silence of three shadows.

It was the silence of a man who had decided to tell the truth, even if the truth destroyed him.

**CHAPTER 2**

**A Road in the Rain**

Let me begin again.

The third day of my story begins where I left it — in a carriage, on the road from the Maer's estates, with the rain falling like a curtain around us. I was traveling south, toward the old holdings of the Lackless family, and I had a letter in my coat that was worth more than the carriage I rode in.

I was thinking of a door.

Not the four-plate door at the University, though I had thought of that one often enough. Not the Lackless door, though I had dreamed of it every night since I left the Maer's company. I was thinking of a door I had seen only once, in a place I had never been, in a dream that did not belong to me.

I was thinking of the door in the old stone, the one that had no handle, no lock, no hinges, no mark of any kind. The door that stood in the middle of a forest where no forest should be, in the shadow of a mountain that did not exist.

But that is a story for later.

For now, I was in a carriage, and the rain was falling, and I had a letter from the Maer tucked into my coat. It was a simple letter — a request, an offer, a promise of payment — and I had already decided what my answer would be.

I would go to the Lackless estate.

I would look for the door.

I would find it, if it existed, and if it did not, I would find the next thing, and the thing after that, until I had followed every thread to its end.

Because that was who I was, in those days. I was a boy who could not stop pulling on threads, even when the threads led to a noose and the noose led to a rope and the rope was already knotted around my neck.

I did not know it yet, but I was riding toward the end of everything I had ever wanted.

---

The carriage was a hired thing, built for function rather than comfort. The seats were hard, the wheels were iron-banded, and the driver was a silent man who spoke only to the horses. I had been traveling for three days when the rain began, and by the fourth day, the road had turned to mud and the sky had turned to gray.

I passed the time by thinking about what I knew.

I knew the Lackless family was old — older than the Maer's line, older than most of the noble houses in Vintas. I knew they were unlucky, though the stories disagreed on whether that was a curse or a reputation or simply a long string of misfortunes that people had woven into a tale. I knew they had a box, and I knew I had held that box in my hands, and I knew it had not opened for me.

But I did not know what was inside it, and I did not know why it would not open, and I did not know why the Maer was so willing to send me to his wife's ancestral home with a letter of introduction and no questions asked.

I was thinking about these things when the carriage hit a hole in the road and I bit my tongue hard enough to taste blood.

"Damn," I said, to no one in particular.

The driver did not respond. The horses did not slow. The rain continued to fall.

I wiped the blood from my mouth with the back of my hand and thought about the Maer's letter. It was brief, but it said more than it wrote:

*To whomever holds the current stewardship of the Lackless lands:*

*The bearer of this letter is a young man of my acquaintance. He has proven himself useful in matters both mundane and arcane. He will require access to the old library, the family archives, and the underground cellars. He may ask questions that seem strange. He may request access to things that have been locked away. He is to be given what he asks for, within reason. If you find his requests unreasonable, send word to me, and I will decide.*

*— Alveron, Maer of Vintas*

I had read the letter enough times to memorize it. I had folded and unfolded it so many times that the creases were soft as cloth. I had wondered, each time I read it, what the Maer was not saying.

The Maer was a man who said exactly what he meant, but only half of what he knew. I had learned this about him during the months I spent in his service. He gave you the pieces of the puzzle, but he never told you what picture they made. He trusted you to see it yourself, or to fail.

I did not intend to fail.

---

I arrived at the Lackless estate on the evening of the fifth day.

The rain had stopped sometime that morning, and the sun had come out long enough to dry the road, but the land itself was wet and heavy. The trees were old and thick, their branches tangled overhead like the veins of a sleeping giant. The road curved through them, following the line of a low stone wall that had been built so long ago that the mortar had turned to moss.

The estate itself was not a castle. It was a manor — old, sprawling, built of gray stone that had weathered centuries of wind and rain. The windows were narrow and few. The roof was steep and dark. The gate was iron, and it stood open, but not invitingly.

I stood in the road and looked at it for a long time.

There was a shape to the place that I did not like. It was not wrong, exactly. It was old in a way that went beyond age. It was old in a way that made the air feel heavier, the light feel thinner, the shadows feel deeper than they should have been.

I had grown up on the road, and I had learned to read places the way other people read faces. A place that has been loved feels warm. A place that has been abandoned feels empty. A place that has been guarded feels closed.

This place did not feel closed. It felt *held* — held shut, held down, held together by something that did not want to let anything in.

I walked through the gate.

---

The steward met me at the door.

He was a tall man, thin, with hair that had once been brown and was now more gray than not. His eyes were pale and his hands were clean and he spoke with the careful, neutral voice of a man who had spent his life delivering news he did not care about.

"Welcome to the Lackless estate," he said. "I am Stevorin. I serve the Lady Lackless's interests here, in her absence."

He did not offer to shake my hand. He did not offer to take my coat. He simply stood in the doorway and looked at me, and I had the distinct impression that he was not pleased to see me.

I gave him the Maer's letter.

He read it. His expression did not change. But his thumb pressed against the paper a little harder than it needed to, and when he looked up, his eyes were a shade colder.

"These are unusual requests," he said.

"I am an unusual young man," I said.

He did not smile. "The old library is in the west wing. The family archives are in the east basement. The underground cellars are beneath the main hall. I will have rooms prepared for you in the north tower. Dinner is served at the seventh hour. I recommend you do not wander after dark. The estate is large, and the passages are not well lit."

He turned and walked away, and I watched him go, and I thought: *That is the face of a man who is hiding something.*

I had seen that face before. I had seen it on Ambrose, when I asked about the four-plate door. I had seen it on the Maer, when I asked about the Amyr. I had seen it on every person I had ever asked about the Chandrian.

It was the face of a man who knew something and did not want to say it.

I smiled, and I did not hide my smile, because there was no one left in the hall to see it.

I had come to the Lackless estate to find a door.

I had a feeling I was going to find more than that.

*End of Chapter 2*
